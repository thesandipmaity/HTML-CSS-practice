Uttarakhand

Uttarakhand, formerly known as Uttaranchal (The official name until 2007), is a state in northern India. The state is bordered by Himachal Pradesh to the northwest, Tibet to the north, Nepal to the east, Uttar Pradesh to the south and southeast, with a small part touching Haryana in the west. Uttarakhand has a total area of 53,566 km2 (20,682 sq mi), equal to 1.6 per cent of the total area of India. Dehradun serves as the state capital, with Nainital being the judicial capital. The state is divided into two divisions, Garhwal and Kumaon, with a total of 13 districts. The forest cover in the state is 45.4 per cent of the state's geographical area. The cultivable area is 16 per cent of the total geographical area. The two major rivers of the state, the Ganges and its tributary Yamuna, originate from the Gangotri and Yamunotri glaciers respectively.

Uttarakhand's name is derived from the Sanskrit words uttara (उत्तर) meaning 'north', and khaṇḍa (खण्ड) meaning 'section' or 'part', altogether simply meaning 'Northern Part'. The name finds mention in early Hindu scriptures as the combined region of "Kedarkhand" (present day Garhwal) and "Manaskhand" (present day Kumaon). Uttarakhand was also the ancient Puranic term for the central stretch of the Indian Himalayas.

Haven in Land

Tourism

Uttarakhand, nestled in the Himalayas, is renowned for its natural beauty and spiritual significance. The state features ancient temples, national parks, hill stations, and mountain peaks, attracting numerous tourists. It is home to 44 nationally protected monuments, with Oak Grove School on the tentative list for World Heritage Sites.

Known as the "Land of the Gods," Uttarakhand is a key pilgrimage destination with sacred sites like Gangotri and Yamunotri (sources of the Ganges and Yamuna), Badrinath, and Kedarnath, forming the Chota Char Dham circuit. Haridwar, hosting the Kumbh Mela, and Rishikesh, a major yoga center, further enhance its spiritual appeal. The state also caters to other religions, including Muslims, Sikhs, and Buddhists.

Adventure enthusiasts can enjoy skiing in Auli and Munsiari, and explore wildlife in 12 national parks and sanctuaries, including the historic Jim Corbett National Park. Activities like trekking, mountaineering, whitewater rafting, and paragliding are popular. Notable sites include Roopkund Lake with its mysterious skeletons and Tehri Dam, the tallest in India, with Tehri Lake offering various water sports.

Spritual

Land of Gods

Inhabitants of the state are called either Garhwali or Kumaoni depending on their region of origin. Hinduism practised by more than three-fourths of the population, with Islam being the next-largest religious group. Hindi is the most widely spoken language and is also the official language of the state, along with native regional languages include Garhwali, Jaunsari and Kumaoni. The state is often referred to as the "Devbhumi" (lit. 'Land of the Gods'), due to its religious significance and numerous Hindu temples and pilgrimage centres found throughout the state. Along with several historical, natural and religious tourist destinations, including Char Dham, Haridwar, Rishikesh, Panch Kedar, Himalayas, and Sapta Badri. Uttarakhand is also home to two World Heritage sites.

Uttarakhand, known as the "Land of the Gods," is renowned for its significant Hindu pilgrimage sites, including Gangotri and Yamunotri (sources of the Ganges and Yamuna), Badrinath, and Kedarnath, which together form the Chota Char Dham pilgrimage circuit. Haridwar, famous for the Kumbh Mela, and Rishikesh, a major yoga center, are also key spiritual destinations. The state hosts various temples dedicated to Hindu deities, and is also a pilgrimage site for Muslims, Sikhs, and Buddhists, with notable sites like Piran Kaliyar Sharif and several Gurudwaras. Additionally, Auli and Munsiari are popular skiing resorts.
